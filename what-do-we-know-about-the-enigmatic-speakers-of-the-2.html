The Mummies Who Spoke an Impossible Language

How ancient DNA is rewriting the story of the most geographically isolated branch of the Indo-European family tree — and raising more questions than it answers.

Sometime around 600 CE, Buddhist scribes in an oasis town on the northern rim of the Tarim Basin wrote down prayers and administrative texts in a language unlike anything their Chinese or Sogdian neighbors would have recognized. The script was borrowed from India, but the language itself — Tocharian — was unmistakably Indo-European, more closely related to Latin and Celtic than to the Iranian tongues spoken by their steppe neighbors to the north and west. For linguists, this was deeply strange. The Tocharian speakers were, geographically, the furthest east of any Indo-European people. Yet by every measure of comparative grammar and vocabulary, their language was among the oldest-branching. How did speakers of a western-flavored language end up isolated in one of the most remote corners of Central Asia?

The answer, it turns out, involves mummies, ancient DNA, and a three-thousand-year gap that nobody has quite managed to fill.

The Indo-European problem is one of the oldest puzzles in historical linguistics: where did this vast family of languages come from, and how did it spread so far so fast? The dominant scientific consensus, hardened by a decade of ancient genomics, points to the Pontic-Caspian steppe — the vast grasslands north of the Black Sea and Caspian — as the homeland of Proto-Indo-European, spoken by mobile pastoralists sometime around 4500–3500 BCE. Lazaridis et al. (2025), in a landmark synthesis, showed that the Yamnaya culture, which coalesced on these steppes around 3300 BCE, drew roughly four-fifths of its ancestry from a group they call the Caucasus-lower Volga (CLV) people. As the authors put it: "The CLV people contributed around four-fifths of the ancestry of the Yamnaya." From this genetic and linguistic fountainhead, populations spread in multiple directions — westward into Europe as Corded Ware and Bell Beaker cultures, southward into South Asia as the ancestors of Sanskrit speakers, and, crucially for our story, eastward into the Altai-Sayan mountain system of southern Siberia.

That eastward movement is embodied in the Afanasievo culture, dated radiocarbon-precisely to roughly 3300–2500 BCE, which occupied what is now the Minusinsk Basin and adjacent Altai highlands. Craniometric analysis by Solodovnikov & Faifert (2024) found that Afanasievo skulls show their greatest similarity to Yamnaya craniological series from the Volga-Ural steppe region — exactly what you would expect if Afanasievo people were migrants from the western steppe. Ancient DNA confirms this picture directly. Zhang et al. (2021) showed that Early Bronze Age individuals from the Dzungarian Basin — the gateway to the Tarim — dated to 3000–2800 BCE, "exhibit a predominantly Afanasievo ancestry with an additional local contribution." The Afanasievo people were, genetically, Yamnaya who had walked east.

Linguists had independently arrived at a similar conclusion. Kassian et al. (2021) placed Tocharian as a sequential outlier from core Indo-European, branching before the inner IE radiation they date to roughly 3357–2162 BCE, consistent with an early eastward departure from a proto-steppe community before the main radiation began. Anthony (2023) noted that between 3000 and 2500 BCE, "populations derived genetically from individuals assigned to the Yamnaya archaeological culture migrated out of their steppe homeland eastward to the Altai Mountains and westward into the Hungarian Plain and southeastern Europe, an east-west range of 5,000 km." The linguistic and genetic arrows pointed at the same target: Tocharian was the linguistic fossil of the Afanasievo migration, the easternmost arm of the great steppe diaspora.

Here is where the story gets genuinely complicated — and where the science is most honest about its own limits.

The Afanasievo culture disappears from the archaeological record around 2500 BCE. Attested Tocharian texts begin around 600 CE. That is a gap of approximately three thousand years, and almost no one working on this problem pretends it has been filled. The mummies of the Tarim Basin — many of them strikingly "western" in appearance, with reddish or brown hair and woolen textiles that look more like Bronze Age Europe than Bronze Age China — have long been assumed to bridge this gap, but the genetics delivered a surprise. Zhang et al. (2021) found that the earliest Tarim Basin individuals, associated with the Xiaohe culture around 2000 BCE, form a genetically isolated population with Ancient North Eurasian (ANE)-like ancestry that is distinct from Afanasievo. They are not simply Afanasievo people who settled the desert oases. They appear to be a separate, highly drifted population whose origins remain debated.

This finding created a genuine disagreement in the field, one that the evidence has not yet resolved. On one side, Bjørn (2022) argued that "the Indo-European identity of the Afanasievo culture finds linguistic substantiation, which adds further weight to the proposition that Tocharian languages derive from this early migration." On the other, the genomic isolation of the early Tarim mummies from the Afanasievo suggests either that the Afanasievo-to-Tocharian connection was mediated by intermediate populations now unknown to us, or that the Xiaohe people arrived in the Tarim from a different direction entirely. Ning et al. (2019) found that Iron Age individuals from Shirenzigou — a site in the Tianshan associated with the period just before attested Tocharian — showed roughly 20–80% Yamnaya-like Western Eurasian ancestry, which is at least consistent with an eventual Afanasievo-derived presence in the region. But the sample size (fewer than ten individuals) makes firm conclusions difficult.

The most recent genomic work from Xinjiang adds texture without resolving the core question. X et al. (2026a) found that during the Iron Age, eastern Tianshan populations drew on a mix of local Tarim ancestry, East Asian millet farmers, and "Western Steppe herders characterized by Afanasievo." J et al. (2025), studying the Deer Stone-Khirgisuur Complex in Mongolia, traced minor western Eurasian ancestry in those populations "back to the Eneolithic/Early Bronze Age Afanasievo and Early Bronze Age Khemtseg (Chemurchek) populations" — suggesting that Afanasievo genetic signals persisted in the region, filtered through intermediate groups, for a very long time.

The linguistic side of the Tocharian puzzle adds another layer of strangeness. Tocharian is not just geographically anomalous — it is typologically bizarre for an Indo-European language. Peyrot (2019) argued that "Tocharian agglutinative case inflexion as well as its single series of voiceless stops, the two most striking typological deviations from Proto-Indo-European, can be explained through influence from Uralic." In other words, if Peyrot is right, Tocharian's odd grammar reflects centuries or millennia of contact with Uralic-speaking populations — the ancestors of today's Finnish and Samoyedic speakers — as the proto-Tocharians migrated eastward across the northern Eurasian forest-steppe zone. This hypothesis is contested: Kassian et al. (2021) and Kroonen et al. (2022) favor internal Indo-European explanations for Tocharian's divergences, viewing them as consequences of geographic isolation rather than contact. The debate hinges on whether the typological features are the kind that spread across linguistic boundaries or the kind that develop internally — a methodological disagreement as much as an empirical one.

Bjørn (2022) assembled evidence for Indo-European loanwords into Uralic, Turkic, and Old Chinese that point specifically to Afanasievo-era contacts: words for "seven," "name/fame," "sister-in-law," "honey," "metal," and "horse" show patterns of borrowing consistent with an Indo-European-speaking community in the Altai-Sayan region during the third millennium BCE. As the paper notes, "Afanasievo culture brought Bronze Age technology to the Altai-Sayan region via Indo-European speakers." This is, admittedly, circumstantial — loanwords can travel through intermediaries — but the pattern is consistent with a long-term Afanasievo presence in the region that left linguistic traces even as the people themselves became genetically diluted or transformed.

Kroonen et al. (2018) made an important negative observation: despite the Hittites speaking an Indo-European language (the earliest attested branch, Anatolian), Bronze Age Anatolia shows "no evidence of direct steppe admixture," leading them to conclude that "the lack of genetic indications for an intrusion into Anatolia refutes the classical notion of a Yamnaya-derived mass invasion or conquest." This matters for Tocharian studies because it establishes a pattern: the archaeological and genetic record of Indo-European spread is not always a story of mass population replacement. Sometimes languages traveled with small groups of mobile elites, pastoralists, or traders whose genetic signal was swamped by the populations they interacted with. The three-thousand-year gap in the Tocharian record may not be empty — it may just be archaeologically invisible.

What does all of this actually tell us about the Tocharian speakers themselves — the people who wrote those 7th-century manuscripts?

The honest answer is: not very much, directly. The genetic evidence from sites contemporary with attested Tocharian is sparse. Dai et al. (2022) found that modern Uyghur populations carry a genetic echo of the ancient Tarim mummies, suggesting long-term continuity in the region, but the specific ancestry of the manuscript-writing Tocharians remains unstudied at the genomic level. The clearest thing the accumulated evidence shows is structural: Tocharian was almost certainly the linguistic product of the Afanasievo migration, a human community that left the western steppe around 3300–3000 BCE, settled the Altai-Sayan highlands, and eventually found its way into the Tarim Basin through pathways that involved intermediate populations — possibly Chemurchek, possibly others — that mixed with, diluted, and eventually replaced the original Afanasievo genetic signal while preserving an Indo-European language.

Key Insight: The Tocharian case is a reminder that language and genes are not the same thing. A population can lose most of its ancestral genetic signal while retaining its language — and the reverse is equally true. The three-thousand-year gap between Afanasievo's disappearance and attested Tocharian is not a problem to be solved by finding a continuous genetic thread. It is an invitation to think more carefully about how languages survive when populations transform.

The most productive next steps are probably genomic: high-coverage sequencing of Iron Age and early medieval individuals from the Tarim Basin and adjacent Tianshan, timed to overlap with the period when Tocharian was actually spoken. Shirenzigou, the only site where this has been partially attempted (Ning et al., 2019), yielded fewer than ten individuals. A systematic effort targeting the 500 BCE–800 CE window — the crucial transition from Bronze Age steppe pastoralism to oasis-dwelling manuscript culture — would either confirm the Afanasievo-derived ancestry hypothesis or force a radical rethinking of Tocharian origins. Until then, the enigmatic speakers of the world's most isolated Indo-European languages remain exactly that: enigmatic.
